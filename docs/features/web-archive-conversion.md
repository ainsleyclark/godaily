# Web Archive as a Conversion Asset

GoDaily already renders every past issue at `/issues/{slug}/` and a
searchable feed at `/browse/`. Today those pages exist mainly to let
people read the archive. The opportunity is to treat them as the
front door: the place where strangers discover GoDaily for the first
time and decide to subscribe.

## Why this exists

At the current stage, the binding constraint on growth is not virality
— it is discovery. Most people who would love a daily Go digest have
simply never heard of it. The archive is the one part of the product
that works while nobody is watching: every issue published is a page
that can be found, shared, and ranked, indefinitely, with no further
effort. Each new issue widens the surface area through which someone
can stumble in.

The goal is to make sure that when someone does land on an archived
issue — from a search engine, a shared link, or a social post — two
things are true: the page is genuinely worth reading on its own, and
subscribing is the obvious next step.

## The end result

- **Every archived issue earns its own discovery.** When someone
  searches for a Go release, a proposal, or a topic GoDaily has covered,
  an issue page is a credible result, and the page that greets them
  looks complete and trustworthy — not like a fragment of an email.
- **Shared links look like a product, not a leak.** A pasted issue link
  in Slack, a forum, or social renders with a clear title, summary, and
  image, so it invites a click instead of looking like spam.
- **Reading converts to subscribing.** A reader who finishes an archived
  issue is offered an unobtrusive, in-content way to get the next one in
  their inbox — no popup, no nag, just the natural next step for someone
  who already enjoyed what they read.
- **Growth compounds quietly.** More issues means more indexed pages
  means more inbound landings, with no ongoing campaign to run. The
  archive becomes an asset that appreciates instead of a cost that
  recurs.

## What success looks like

The share of new subscribers who arrive via the archive (rather than the
homepage alone) climbs over time, and it climbs without anyone spending
effort each week to make it happen. Archived issues begin appearing for
relevant searches, and the conversion rate from "landed on an issue" to
"subscribed" is high enough that publishing more issues is, by itself, a
growth lever.

## What this is not

This is not a popup strategy or an aggressive capture funnel. It honours
the brand promise — free, no tracking pixels, no dark patterns. The
archive earns subscribers by being useful and easy to act on, not by
interrupting the reader.
